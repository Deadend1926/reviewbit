Get Paid To Learn: 10 Jobs That Offer Paid Training
Looking for a way to build your career and improve your resume without going broke in the process? You’re not alone. Thousands of people are now choosing jobs that pay for training, letting them earn money to learn valuable skills while working.
This guide covers real job opportunities that pay you while you train – no skills or college degree required. Whether you’re looking to upskill, switch industries, or start fresh, you’ll find options here that support both your growth and your wallet.
And if you’re in a hurry to earn money, some of these roles let you start almost immediately.

1. Paid digital marketing training programs

Digital marketing is one of the fastest-growing fields, and the best part is – you don’t need a degree to break in. Many programs now pay for learning through bootcamps, internships, or on-the-job training models.
What you can learn:
  • SEO, PPC, and email marketing basics
  • Social media strategies
  • Analytics and reporting tools like Google Analytics
Roles often include:
  • PPC assistant
  • Social media coordinator
  • Email marketing trainee
Where to find opportunities:
  • Acadium offers apprenticeships for beginners
  • Marketing agencies often run paid internships 
Tips:
  • Search for businesses offering “junior marketing roles with training”
  • Look for listings labeled “marketing trainee” or “digital marketing apprentice”
  • These roles are perfect for students looking to build a resume early

2. IT and tech support roles with on-the-job training

If you're interested in technology, help desk and support roles are great entry points. Many businesses train new hires in everything from system setup to troubleshooting, and they pay you while you learn.
Common roles include:
  • Junior IT technician
  • Help desk assistant
  • Tech support specialist
What you’ll learn:
  • Networking basics
  • Software and hardware troubleshooting
  • Security protocols
  • Ticketing systems 
Bonus: Many employers also cover certifications like CompTIA A+ or Microsoft Fundamentals.
Where to look:
  • Managed service providers (MSPs) often hire people without prior work experience
  • Look into government-funded IT training programs in your area
  • Virtual assistant platforms sometimes offer tech-support style roles, too
This path is ideal for people with strong problem-solving skills and seeking to succeed in technology – no degree or prior work experience is required.

3. Healthcare assistant and CNA programs

The healthcare field is full of job opportunities for people who want to earn while they learn. Certified Nursing Assistant (CNA) and healthcare aide training programs often come with a paycheck from day one.
Where you’ll find them:
  • Hospitals and nursing homes
  • Community colleges with a paid CNA program
  • State-funded programs that support workforce transitions
What to expect:
  • Programs last 4–12 weeks
  • CPR certification and basic patient care classes
  • Background check and physical may be required
  • A clear path into long-term healthcare roles with advancement
The best part? These jobs often include benefits, guaranteed placement, and upward mobility, making them perfect for people starting over or changing careers.

4. Apprenticeships in construction and skilled trades

A skilled trade apprenticeship could be perfect if you like hands-on work. These jobs pay you while you learn directly from experienced tradespeople.
Common paths:
  • Electrician
  • Plumber
  • HVAC technician
  • Carpenter
You’ll learn:
  • Safety protocols
  • Tool and equipment handling
  • Installation and repair techniques
  • Project planning and code compliance
Why it’s worth it:
  • Starting pay ranges from $15–$25/hour
  • Most programs last 1–4 years
  • You graduate as a licensed professional
  • Strong career path with high earning potential
These are some of the most well-established jobs, often backed by unions or state programs.

5. Customer service roles with internal training

Customer service is a great place to start if you’re looking for a remote-friendly, beginner-level job. Many businesses teach you as you go for call center or online support roles.
What skills you’ll learn:
  • CRM systems (like Salesforce or Zendesk)
  • Conflict resolution and empathy techniques
  • Phone and chat support protocols
  • Company policies and product knowledge
These jobs are ideal if:
  • You want to work from home
  • You are building soft skills for future career advancement
  • You need flexible hours or transportation-friendly options
Some employers also train you in high-income skills like upselling, customer retention, and service analytics – a great bonus on your resume.

6. Paid sales development representative (SDR) roles

Sales is a great field if you enjoy talking to people and solving problems – and the best part is, many businesses pay you to learn the ropes.
Typical entry-level roles:
  • Sales Development Representative (SDR)
  • Business Development Rep (BDR)
  • Sales apprentice or intern
What you’ll learn:
  • CRM and sales pipeline tools
  • Cold email and outreach techniques
  • Lead qualification and follow-up
  • Communication and objection handling
Benefits of this path:
  • Most SDRs earn $40k–$60k base + commission
  • Strong career growth into an account executive or marketing roles
  • Some businesses offer remote training and promote quickly
This is a popular route for college grads, but many teams welcome those without a degree or work skills as long as they bring energy and curiosity.

7. Paid coding bootcamps with job guarantees

Tech bootcamps are a fast track into technology careers – and some pay for training or offer income-share agreements (ISAs), where you don’t pay until you land a job. A few even offer stipends while you learn.
Top programs to explore:
  • Revature – pays trainees during the coding bootcamp
  • Reskill America – tuition-free coding classes
What skills you’ll learn:
  • HTML, CSS, JavaScript
  • QA testing and debugging
  • Git, APIs, and version control
  • Real project work with mentor support
Career paths include junior developer, QA tester, or front-end engineer. Just be cautious: avoid programs that charge high fees without offering job opportunities or job guarantees.
If you’re more interested in product testing, you can also become a product tester – some roles even include entry-level tech skills like UI testing.

8. Beauty and personal care apprenticeships

Love beauty, hair, or skincare? Many salons offer training programs where you get licensed while earning. These roles let you gain skills and build a client base before going solo or opening your own business.
Types of trainings:
  • Hairdressing or barbering
  • Esthetics and skincare
  • Nail technician
What to look for:
  • Licensing is included in training
  • Duration (typically 6 months to 2 years)
  • Hourly wage or stipend
  • Mentorship and client exposure
You’ll learn these skills:
  • Sanitation and safety
  • Color techniques, styling, and treatments
  • Customer service and booking systems
If beauty is your hobby, this is one hobby that pays – master the craft, and it can become a full-time career.

  • Get started with no degree or prior training

10. Government-backed job training programs

Many countries offer job opportunities that pay for learning through public programs. In the U.S., the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) helps adults access subsidized career coaching and apprenticeships in high-demand fields.
These programs support:
  • Career changers
  • Unemployed workers
  • People without a college degree or formal work experience
What’s covered:
  • Tuition or course fees
  • Transportation or living stipends
  • Internships and paid placement programs
Where to look:
  • CareerOneStop.org
  • Local workforce development offices
  • Government labor department sites in your country
This path is ideal for people looking for long-term growth opportunities with real benefits and employer support.

Final thoughts

You don’t need to choose between income and education. These jobs let you grow, gain skills, and earn all at the same time. Whether you’re going into tech, trades, or something totally new, there’s a path that fits your goals.
Some options are in-person, others are fully remote. All of them help you move forward with confidence, even if you don’t have a degree or years of work experience.
Explore these growth opportunities, apply, and start building a career that works for your lifestyle.
